Senior High School Elective Chemistry - Year 1

Section 1: Introduction to Chemistry

Unit 1: Chemistry as a Discipline

Chemistry is the scientific study of matter, its properties, and how it interacts with other substances. It plays a crucial role in our daily lives and is fundamental to understanding the world around us.

1.1 Definition and Scope of Chemistry

Chemistry encompasses the study of elements, compounds, and the reactions between them. It investigates the composition, structure, properties, and changes of matter at the atomic and molecular level.

1.2 Branches of Chemistry

  • Organic Chemistry: Study of carbon-containing compounds
  • Inorganic Chemistry: Study of non-carbon-containing compounds
  • Physical Chemistry: Study of the physical properties of chemical systems
  • Analytical Chemistry: Study of the composition of substances
  • Biochemistry: Study of chemical processes in living organisms

1.3 Importance of Chemistry in Daily Life

Chemistry impacts various aspects of our lives, including:

  • Medicine and healthcare
  • Food and agriculture
  • Environmental science
  • Materials science and technology
  • Energy production and storage

Unit 2: Measurement of Physical Quantities

2.1 SI Units and Fundamental Quantities

The International System of Units (SI) provides a standardized system for measurement. Fundamental quantities include:

  • Length (meter, m)
  • Mass (kilogram, kg)
  • Time (second, s)
  • Temperature (Kelvin, K)
  • Amount of substance (mole, mol)
  • Electric current (ampere, A)
  • Luminous intensity (candela, cd)

2.2 Derived Units

Derived units are formed by combining fundamental units. Examples include:

  • Velocity (m/s)
  • Acceleration (m/s²)
  • Force (Newton, N = kg·m/s²)
  • Energy (Joule, J = N·m)
  • Pressure (Pascal, Pa = N/m²)

2.3 Significant Figures and Scientific Notation

Significant figures represent the precision of a measurement. Scientific notation is used to express very large or very small numbers concisely.

Unit 3: Basic Safety Laboratory Practices

3.1 General Laboratory Safety Rules

  • Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE)
  • Never eat, drink, or smoke in the laboratory
  • Know the location of safety equipment (fire extinguishers, eyewash stations, etc.)
  • Report all accidents immediately

3.2 Handling Chemicals

  • Read and understand Safety Data Sheets (SDS) before handling chemicals
  • Use proper techniques for transferring chemicals
  • Dispose of chemicals properly according to regulations

3.3 Laboratory Equipment Safety

  • Proper use and handling of glassware
  • Safe operation of Bunsen burners and heating devices
  • Correct use of fume hoods for handling volatile substances

Work Solutions

Formula: Density = Mass / Volume

Example questions and solutions:

  1. Question: A cube of metal has a mass of 54 grams and a volume of 6 cm³. Calculate its density.

    Solution:

    Given:

    • Mass (m) = 54 g
    • Volume (V) = 6 cm³

    Using the formula: Density = Mass / Volume

    Density = 54 g / 6 cm³ = 9 g/cm³

  2. Question: A liquid has a density of 0.8 g/mL. What is the mass of 250 mL of this liquid?

    Solution:

    Given:

    • Density (ρ) = 0.8 g/mL
    • Volume (V) = 250 mL

    Rearranging the formula: Mass = Density × Volume

    Mass = 0.8 g/mL × 250 mL = 200 g

  3. Question: A piece of wood has a mass of 25 g and a density of 0.5 g/cm³. What is its volume?

    Solution:

    Given:

    • Mass (m) = 25 g
    • Density (ρ) = 0.5 g/cm³

    Rearranging the formula: Volume = Mass / Density

    Volume = 25 g / 0.5 g/cm³ = 50 cm³

  4. Question: An object has a mass of 150 g and a volume of 30 cm³. Is it more or less dense than water (density of water = 1 g/cm³)?

    Solution:

    Given:

    • Mass (m) = 150 g
    • Volume (V) = 30 cm³

    Calculate the density: Density = Mass / Volume

    Density = 150 g / 30 cm³ = 5 g/cm³

    Since 5 g/cm³ is greater than 1 g/cm³, the object is more dense than water.

  5. Question: A gas has a density of 1.25 kg/m³ at room temperature. What is its density in g/L?

    Solution:

    Given:

    • Density = 1.25 kg/m³

    Convert kg/m³ to g/L:

    1 kg = 1000 g

    1 m³ = 1000 L

    1.25 kg/m³ = (1.25 × 1000 g) / 1000 L = 1.25 g/L

Projects

Project 1: Density Column

Materials Needed:
  • Tall, clear glass or plastic container
  • Honey
  • Dish soap
  • Water
  • Vegetable oil
  • Rubbing alcohol
  • Food coloring (optional)
  • Small objects of different materials (e.g., grape, cherry tomato, ping pong ball, metal nut)
Steps:
  1. Pour honey into the container, filling about 1/5 of its height.
  2. Slowly pour dish soap down the side of the container, creating a second layer.
  3. Mix water with food coloring (if using) and carefully pour it down the side of the container to form the third layer.
  4. Slowly pour vegetable oil to create the fourth layer.
  5. Finally, pour rubbing alcohol to form the top layer.
  6. Observe the distinct layers formed due to differences in density.
  7. Gently drop the small objects into the column and observe where they settle.

This project demonstrates the concept of density and how different liquids and solids interact based on their densities.

Project 2: Creating a Simple Spectroscope

Materials Needed:
  • Empty paper towel roll or a small cardboard box
  • CD or DVD (old, unusable one)
  • Scissors
  • Tape
  • Black paper
  • Various light sources (e.g., incandescent bulb, fluorescent light, LED)
Steps:
  1. Cut a small slit (about 1 cm wide) at one end of the paper towel roll or make a small hole in one side of the box.
  2. On the opposite end, cut a larger opening (about 3 cm x 3 cm).
  3. Cut a small piece of the CD or DVD (about 2 cm x 2 cm) that includes part of the reflective surface.
  4. Tape this piece over the larger opening, with the reflective side facing inward.
  5. Cover any remaining openings with black paper to block external light.
  6. Point the slit end towards different light sources and look through the CD/DVD piece to observe the spectrum.

This project introduces students to the concept of light spectra and how different light sources produce different spectral patterns.
